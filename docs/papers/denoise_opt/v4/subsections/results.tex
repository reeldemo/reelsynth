\section{Results}
\label{sec:results}

\paragraph{Family-conditional hardness (completed bench).}
On the fitted DenoiseOpt 100\,000-cycle bench, denoise score $D$ is lowest for \texttt{nonlinear} ($D\approx 0.39$) and \texttt{combo} ($D\approx 0.40$), then \texttt{triple\_mix} / \texttt{extreme\_overlay} ($D\approx 0.51$--$0.52$), while \texttt{harmonic\_fft} and \texttt{am\_fm} sit near $D\approx 0.69$--$0.70$.
Lit-combo champion family stress (prolonged residual) similarly ranks \texttt{open\_wrap\_bias} ($R\approx 0.83$) and \texttt{extreme\_overlay} / \texttt{combo} ($R\approx 0.88$--$0.89$) below \texttt{harmonic\_fft} ($R\approx 0.95$).
Hard sounds recur; they are not a random one-off.

\paragraph{Overnight hybrid (interim).}
Final mean-$R$ tables will be ingested after the overnight campaign reaches its declared iteration budget.
Monitoring plots below are for protocol transparency only; incomplete-run champions are \emph{not} asserted as finished science.

\paragraph{Inference time at matched score.}
Across diverse high-$R$ fitted cells ($R\ge 0.98$), we timed CUDA forward passes (batch 64, prolonged residual).
Within a tight band of the best residual ($R \ge R_{\max}-5\cdot10^{-4}$), the \textbf{favorite} is the lowest-latency model:
\texttt{champion\_iter\_000235} with $R\approx 0.9910$, $\approx 3.15$\,ms/batch, $\approx 37$k parameters
(versus the absolute max-$R$ cell at $R\approx 0.9914$ but $\approx 7.2$\,ms/batch / $\approx 104$k params).
Selection rule: maximize $R$, then minimize latency inside the band.

\subsection{Top-5 architectures (current leaderboard snapshot)}
\label{sec:top5-arch}
This subsection ranks five \emph{distinct} fitted bake graphs from the CUDA inference bench
(\texttt{inference\_bench.json}; batch 64), preferring residual $R$ first and latency on ties,
and skipping near-duplicate copies of the same block list.
The overnight GPU hybrid was still interim at the time of writing (monitor $\approx 5$k iterations,
champion $R\approx 0.991$); numbers below are a \textbf{current leaderboard snapshot}, not a finished
mean-$R$ claim after the declared budget.

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{\texttt{champion\_iter\_000235\_fitted}} (favorite).
    Compact dual-block graph \texttt{[tf\_split, unet]}, depth~$12$, width~$40$,
    classical bake ops \texttt{[pin, cycle\_net]}, cell \texttt{tf\_split}.
    $\approx 37{,}489$ params; live $R\approx 0.9915$ (saved $\approx 0.9910$);
    $\approx 3.15$\,ms/batch.
    Ranks first on live residual \emph{and} is the latency favorite inside the near-max-$R$ band.

  \item \textbf{\texttt{champion\_iter\_000140\_fitted}}.
    Full MoE stack \texttt{[tf\_split, unet, moe\_mix, residual, noise\_cond, mlp]},
    depth~$12$, width~$32$, ops \texttt{[fade\_pull, pin, polish]}, sequential MoE.
    $\approx 104{,}484$ params; live $R\approx 0.9914$; $\approx 6.69$\,ms/batch.
    Next-best distinct topology: higher capacity than the favorite at roughly $2\times$ latency.

  \item \textbf{\texttt{champion\_iter\_000565\_fitted}}.
    Mid triple \texttt{[tf\_split, unet, moe\_mix]}, depth~$12$, width~$32$,
    ops \texttt{[fade\_pull, cycle\_net, pin, polish]}.
    $\approx 53{,}933$ params; live $R\approx 0.9907$; $\approx 4.11$\,ms/batch.
    Score/latency compromise between the compact dual-block and the full six-block stack.

  \item \textbf{\texttt{champion\_iter\_000205\_fitted}}.
    Same six-block MoE family as (2) but narrow width~$24$ (depth~$12$),
    ops \texttt{[fade\_pull, cycle\_net, pin, polish]}.
    $\approx 70{,}562$ params; live $R\approx 0.9907$; $\approx 7.29$\,ms/batch.
    Capacity variant: fewer parameters than (2) without a latency win on this bench.

  \item \textbf{\texttt{champion\_iter\_000192\_fitted}}.
    Reordered triple \texttt{[tf\_split, moe\_mix, unet]}, depth~$12$, width~$32$,
    ops \texttt{[fade\_pull, cycle\_net, pin, polish]}.
    $\approx 49{,}804$ params; live $R\approx 0.9894$; $\approx 3.98$\,ms/batch.
    Fast alternate block order; trades a small amount of $R$ for near-favorite latency.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{Classical vs AI (same residual protocol).}
On the overnight runner geometry (CUDA, batch 64, prolonged residual $R$), ten non-learnable bake denoisers were timed against the neural favorite above.
DualCosine reproduces the paper baseline at $R\approx 0.820$ ($\approx 1.19$\,ms/batch), consistent with the overnight monitor DualCosine $\approx 0.817$.
The best \emph{active} classical method is a seam-local 3-tap FIR (\texttt{seam\_fir3}) at $R\approx 0.963$ / $\approx 0.60$\,ms/batch; identity (no-op) scores $R\approx 0.974$ and is reported only as a ceiling, not as a denoiser.
The neural favorite reaches $R\approx 0.991$ at $\approx 2.47$\,ms/batch ($\approx 37$k params): $\Delta R \approx {+}0.028$ vs \texttt{seam\_fir3} and $\Delta R \approx {+}0.171$ vs DualCosine, at roughly $4.1\times$ / $2.1\times$ the latency of those classical baselines respectively.
This is a matched-protocol inference bench, \emph{not} a finished overnight mean-$R$ claim.
